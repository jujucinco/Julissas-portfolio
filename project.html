A cloud storage solution for mobile and desktop

The Challenge

Cloud storage apps have become a necessity for many, whether it be for work, school, or to store our massive photo libraries. There are several big names already in this game, most notably, Google, iCloud and Dropbox. When I was asked to create a cloud storage app concept, I saw an opportunity to stand out in a crowded field by catering to a niche market: College Students. I set out to create the kind of collaborative app many of us wish we had in our schooling years, while prioritizing my client needs.

The Process

Understanding Our Users

Survey Results

I conducted a survey to gauge the current use of cloud storage solutions and pain points:

Pain Point #1: Organization is difficult

Users lamented the lack of easy organization options available in their current cloud storage solution. My research into this found the most popular apps among survey participants only provide the option to move a file to a folder and color code it, but no tagging abilities.

Pain Point #2: Lack of storage space

Even Google Drive’s 15GB is not sufficient for an app that encourages sharing and collaboration. With the ability to save all of our phone’s photos to these cloud storage solutions, users expect to not have to constantly remove content in order to upload more.

Pain Point #3: Not easy to use

Some users felt that their current solution had several steps in order to complete a simple action, such as editing a document.

Through this survey, I also found 100% of participants who said they used a cloud storage solution for school said they shared notes with classmates and/or asked classmates to share notes with them. Users are using cloud storage solutions for this purpose, but they are doing so with the popular cloud storage solutions that do not target this specific use case.

Competitive Analysis

In order to understand the market we would be entering into, I conducted a SWOT analysis on the top 3 apps used by my survey participants.Through this analysis, I found weaknesses in the market I could leverage while building this solution.

  • No option to add tags for organization
  • To create content within app, user must have third party app (MS Office) installed
  • Users can only edit in real time in paid version
  • Organization is by folder and color, no tags
  • Inability to easily record audio within the app
  • Web app and desktop app do not have consistent functionality
  • Easy to inadvertently move cards around while scrolling in mobile version

User Stories

To further focus on my user’s goals and put them in perspective, I built user stories based on my user feedback and my client desires.

Some High Priority...

"I want to edit files in real time with other app users to facilitate collaboration and so that edits are not made on an old file"

"I want to create content in my cloud storage solution to prevent having to work in multiple apps"

"I want to share groups of files as well as individual files to increase efficiency"

Some Medium...

Some Low...

“As a college student...”

Sketches and Wireframing

Next, I drew up some rough sketches to get my ideas out on paper and identify weak points. I then used Whimsical to quickly put together some wireframes, and Invision to turn these into a clickable prototype for my usability testing phase.

Usability Testing

Although most were direct successes, usability testing was eye opening for me, as it revealed usability issues with components commonly used across apps. For example the “more” icon, used in order to locate additional actions for a given element, was not immediately noticed by all four of my participants, with one noting that they never really knew what that was used for.

Branding

Name

Through some mind mapping, I arrived at the name ClassKarma. I used the word “Class”, due to being a cloud storage solution for class collaboration, and “Karma” which was a play on the gamification feature I planned to implement to ensure the collaboration and sharing was fair.

Logo

Karma means, what goes around comes around, or effect from cause. I wanted my logo to include some reference to this, while identifying it as a cloud storage solution. I iterated on a series of sketches and arrived at the final logo.

Typography

I chose Fredoka One and Quicksand for their complementary styles, ease of legibility and the emotion they evoked. I was going for a light, fun feel that appealed to my young demographic, and I feel these two typefaces did that beautifully.

Color Palette

I wanted the color palette to feel youthful and interesting, without being overwhelming. I went with accent colors bright sky blue and burnt orange, and a tint of the burnt orange that was used exclusively for the mobile app. The darker grey hue was primarily used for text, while the lighter grey was used more seldom, such as in the desktop footer. I used a significant amount of white space to ensure my design had room to breath.

Buttons

For desktop and mobile buttons, I followed iOS interface guidelines best practices for sizing (minimum of 40px)

Bringing It All Together

I addressed the client needs, our target users main pain points, and a new feature that will set the app apart from competitors. Shown above from left to right:

• Ability to scan. This is a feature not widely offered by competitors, and something 94% of my survey respondents said may be useful to them for scanning hard copy notes.

• Tagging addresses organizational pain point, gives user another option other than organizing by folder or color.

• Offering ample storage to encourage collaboration. Karma points helps address our initial problem of unfair sharing of class notes. Students have to contribute to get karma points and get more access to other student’s notes.

• Ability to easily share single files or entire folders, as requested by the client.

• An easy way to create content right in the app, easy to see who is currently working in a document.

A/B Testing

I did some A/B testing on some final design variations I couldn’t quite decide on, when this happens, I feel confident falling back on the user to decide for me. These tests asked 42 participants to decide between two hero section logos, a mobile log in screen layout, and the placement of “cancel” text.

“Which Design Do You Prefer?”

Although a statistically insignificant margin, The only test with a slight preference with 42 users was the hero section logo. Users did not seem to have a preference for either of the variations for the cancel text placement or log in screen. This was at least reassurance that either of my designs would have been successful. I did a bit more research and went with the design that most closely matched current app design patterns.

More Testing

Testing the high fidelity version brings a lot more fidelity to the test and gives the user a much better sense of what the app could actually do. It’s also reassuring to know your research, testing and iterating process is working because users breeze through the tasks in your high fidelity test. This was also the case in this project, with users completing all tasks easily and efficiently and with minimal feedback.

Final Thoughts

Cloud storage apps are a great solution to get people to collaborate and share with more ease and efficiency. There was a need for a specific market of cloud storage app users that were not having all of their needs met with the current solutions available to them. An important reminder from this project is that the process is essential. Particularly when working with a client who is not sure what direction they want to take the project, making sure each step is done thoughtfully makes everyone’s job easier in the end. And of course, the importance of user testing (and more user testing) can never be emphasized enough. When certain things just don’t work like you think they will, like common icons and language, users are there to ground you and make you take a step back. There are plenty of opportunities to expand features for ClassKarma in future updates (social, chat, classmate invites…) which will make this app even more competitive in this space.
