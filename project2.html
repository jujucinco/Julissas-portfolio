As high speed wifi, video conferencing, synchronous and asynchronous communication has advanced in the last 10 years, remote work has grown exponentially. It’s common to see fully remote workforces, digital nomads who work from their laptops in an exotic location, and coworking spaces on every corner. Along with all the perks, however, comes a major downside. Isolation is a prominent complaint among many location independent employees, which is directly responsible for the rise in coworking spaces. Although lacking a traditional workplace watercooler, the desire to connect for professional networking or just friends remains. In the case of digital nomads, these connections are sometimes even more important, as they are not as easily able to build relationships on the go. As someone who has personally seen coworkers struggle with this, and noticing current apps for this demographic fall short, I set out to bridge this gap myself.

In Every Problem Lies an Opportunity

I had all of the ingredients: the user base, public spaces with wifi, coworking spaces, and the desire and need for an app of this kind. It was just about blending everything together in a unique way that addresses the core problem of isolation when not working in an office environment.

One is a Lonely Number

Although there is loads of survey data on remote workers and digital nomads, I created a survey to confirm my research findings, and narrow down on the most important core features for my MVP.

Some Notable Findings:

  • The top two reasons for wanting to connect with other remote workers was for professional networking (81%) and making friends (76%)

  • Many remote workers try to connect with others through meetups or Facebook groups, but none of my respondents use an app specifically for this demographic.

  • A common frustration (24% of respondents) was being easily distracted in a public space or at home while working remotely. I noted user’s desires to know how quiet a location was as an amenity.

  • The longer someone was remote, the less isolated they reported feeling. This may coincide with just getting used to the isolation, or, with a growing family and therefore support system as they grew older.

Scoping Out The Competition

There are plenty of apps out there to find coworking spaces, or just a coffee shop with wifi. Google maps will get the job done and is most people’s go-to for finding any kind of business anywhere. However I looked for apps that could show me spaces near me and offered some kind of networking feature.

“Find great workspaces & wifi anywhere.”

  • Web app instead of a native app. Experience on iPhone X was not responsive, screen was not fitted properly and laggy performance.
  • Welcome screen had significant amount of text, onboarding and navigation was confusing.
  • Two tab bar screens were identical.
  • Could only see who has been in a particular space, instead of who was currently there.

“A community of creatives, freelancers, and entrepreneurs who work beyond the corporate cubicle.”

  • UX was lacking due to unlabled, unclear tab bar icons and small menu navigation
  • Networking feature was lacking ability to see who was local to you, only all users around the world.
  • Listings were not very detailed.

“Plain Sight helps you find places to go based on the type of professional connections you're looking to make.”

  • Geared more towards professional networking only, versus professional networking and for making friends.
  • Very private, do not offer to show a user’s image and only show user initial. Some may see as a positive but it felt very impersonal.
  • Currently very limited scope, only available in one city.

Test, Rinse, Repeat

User testing the wireframes are the first real glimpse into which of my ideas are working or not. Because the low fidelity of the screens required a quick and dirty method, I went with Maze. Having users test some of the core functions and seeing if they recognized iconography led to some important improvements.

Users did not recognize the placement of a “more” icon placed over an image

Users did not always recognize a notification icon that was not a standard bell icon.

The color and shape of an icon, as well as what elements surround that icon, is deeply influential on whether or not users will even notice the icon is there.

Branding Bread and Butter

Name

I wanted something short and sweet, simple yet descriptive. I went through several iterations of names, until I found the perfect combination that transmitted

      1. You can find spaces here.
      2. It goes where you go, anywhere in the world.
      3. It’s for people open to connecting with others.
    =
    Open Space.

Logo

A large percentage of my target demographic is full time digital nomads, or remote workers that travel while working a significant amount. I worked a globe into the the logo and used a font that was modern and clean.

Typography

Proxima nova was the font of choice for its modern proportions, slight geometric appearance, and variety of weights and widths.

Color Palette

Professional yet casual. Work and play. this is not the easiest balance to attain with color. But this precisely what I wanted to portray on an app that can be used for professional networking or making friends. This combination of light and dark and accessibility friendly hues were able to accomplish that.

Buttons

Rounded corners for added “friendliness” and the perfect size for an iOS mobile app. I used a combination of the color palette to add visual interest.

Sum and Substance

There is a gaping whitespace available to fill for this rapidly growing demographic of remote workers and digital nomads. Many would say distributed teams will be the new normal for businesses, and as Slack becomes the new office , so will concerns of isolation and loneliness. Open Space solves this problem by enabling an easy, fun way to connect with likeminded people who are already in the same place you are. It also solves some secondary problems discovered through my research, such as the frustration of working in a loud space or one with poor wifi.

Something I had to think about throughout the project was the massive amount of user submitted data an app like this requires. It would take time to garner enough data to be able to compete in a space with Workfrom or Workhardanywhere. To overcome this, good UX and offering highly coveted features is essential. A possible opportunity to expand in the future and a feature my survey respondents expressed some interest in, would be the addition of connecting with others for dating, instead of just for professional purposes or friends.
